Do's

• In Case of Injury place the animal in a cool, well ventilated place or a shaded area.Once first aid has been administered, and the animal is stable, consult a vet. Give small amounts of cold water containing glucose or sugar frequently. Ice packs should be applied on the head, forehead and all over the body. Give cold milk to drink. Once first aid has been administered, and the animal is stable, consult a vet.

• Do observe your potential new pet’s behavior. Is he calm and friendly, or stressed and fearful?

• Do let the shelter worker know your situation and ask for recommendations based on your situation.

• Do ask questions. If your considering adopting, you’ll want to know as much about your new pet as the shelter does.

• Do listen to staff and volunteers – they know the animals best.

• Clean their place, walk, socialize, feed, and spend time with them.

Don'ts

• Excess weight puts stress on your pet’s joints and internal organs, putting him at risk for a variety of health problems, including skin infections, heart disease, diabetes, and arthritis.

• Do not abandon your pet.

• Don’t bring unruly or unsupervised children into the animal shelter. Dogs, cats and other animals may be upset by loud noises or fast movement.

• When checking out a pet to determine if he is kid friendly, Don’t let your child pull on the pet’s ears or tail to see how he will react.

• Don’t intimidate, tease, or otherwise “freak out” a animals – like staring him down, making aggressive movements, etc. It’s not funny, it’s not cool. It’s extremely stressful to the animal.

• Don’t feed an animal in the shelter. You could make him sick, or even kill him.

• Don’t adopt a pet as a “gift” to someone who hasn’t expressed the desire for one. He may end up back at the shelter, or worse.
